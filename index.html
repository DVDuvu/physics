Newton's Laws and Basics of Dynamics

Dynamics is a branch of mechanics that studies the mechanical motion of bodies, delving into the causes of its occurrence and change. Unlike kinematics, which describes motion without considering causes, dynamics considers forces as the source of motion.

Basic Concepts

  • Material Point: A model of a body whose dimensions can be neglected. All mass is concentrated at one point.
  • Translational Motion: Motion where any line fixed to the body stays parallel to itself. All points share the same velocity and acceleration.
  • Inertia: The property of bodies to maintain their velocity (magnitude and direction) in the absence of external forces.

Animation: Inertia — ball maintains speed without forces.

Newton's Laws

Newton's laws are fundamental principles of classical mechanics describing motion under forces. They apply to inertial reference frames (IRF).

First Law (Law of Inertia)

In IRFs, a material point remains at rest or in uniform rectilinear motion (\( \vec{v} = \mathrm{const} \)) when no net force acts on it: \( \sum \vec{F} = 0 \).

Earth is a good approximation of an IRF for local processes. Galileo's Principle: In all IRFs, the laws of mechanics are identical.

Animation: Ball in uniform motion (inertia).

Second Law

For a constant-mass point: \[ \sum \vec{F} = m \vec{a} \] Acceleration is proportional to net force and inversely proportional to mass.

Animation: Car accelerating under force.

Third Law

Bodies interact with forces equal in magnitude and opposite in direction: \[ \vec{F_1} = -\vec{F_2} \] The Earth attracts an apple with the same force the apple attracts the Earth.

Hydrostatics (Pressure)

Covers pressure in fluids, Pascal's law, communicating vessels, and mercury calculations — school physics grade 7.

Pressure

\[ P = \frac{F}{S} = \frac{mg}{S} \]

Hydrostatic Pressure

\[ P = \rho g h \] where \(\rho\) is density, \(g \approx 9.8\,\text{m/s}^2\), \(h\) is depth.

Mercury example (\(\rho = 13600\,\text{kg/m}^3,\; h = 0.746\,\text{m}\)): \[ P = 13600 \times 9.8 \times 0.746 \approx 10^5\,\text{Pa} \]

1 atm = 10⁵ Pa  |  1 mmHg = 133.3 Pa  |  760 mmHg ≈ 1 atm

Pascal's Law

Pressure applied to a fluid in a closed container is transmitted equally in all directions. In a hydraulic press: \(\dfrac{F_1}{S_1} = \dfrac{F_2}{S_2}\).

Communicating Vessels

Homogeneous liquid reaches the same level in all connected vessels. For different liquids: \(\rho_1 g h_1 = \rho_2 g h_2\).

Animation: Level equalization in communicating vessels.

Manometer

U-tube with mercury: \(P = P_0 + \rho g \Delta h\). At \(h = 0.76\,\text{m}\): \(P \approx 101325\,\text{Pa}\) (1 atm).

Formulas — Grades 7–10

Grade 7 — Kinematics, Density, Pressure, Forces

  • Speed: \( v = \dfrac{s}{t} \)
  • Acceleration: \( a = \dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \)
  • Density: \( \rho = \dfrac{m}{V} \)
  • Pressure: \( p = \dfrac{F}{S} \)
  • Hydrostatic Pressure: \( p = \rho g h \)
  • Archimedes' Force: \( F_A = \rho_{\text{liq}} g V_{\text{sub}} \)
  • Gravity: \( F_g = mg \)
  • Hooke's Law: \( F = -k\,\Delta l \)

Animation: Spring stretching (Hooke's Law).

Grade 8 — Thermal Phenomena, Electricity Basics

  • Heat: \( Q = cm\Delta T \)
  • Melting heat: \( Q = \lambda m \)
  • Vaporization heat: \( Q = rm \)
  • Joule–Lenz: \( Q = I^2 R t \)
  • Current: \( I = \dfrac{q}{t} \)
  • Ohm's Law: \( I = \dfrac{U}{R} \)

Grade 9 — Dynamics, Energy, Momentum, Gravity

  • Momentum: \( \vec{p} = m\vec{v} \)
  • Impulse: \( \Delta\vec{p} = \vec{F}\,\Delta t \)
  • Conservation of momentum: \( \sum\vec{p}_1 = \sum\vec{p}_2 \)
  • Kinetic energy: \( E_k = \dfrac{mv^2}{2} \)
  • Potential energy: \( E_p = mgh \)
  • Conservation of energy: \( E_{k1} + E_{p1} = E_{k2} + E_{p2} \)
  • Power: \( N = \dfrac{A}{t},\quad A = Fs\cos\alpha \)
  • Universal Gravitation: \( F = G\dfrac{m_1 m_2}{r^2},\quad G = 6.67\times10^{-11}\,\text{N·m}^2/\text{kg}^2 \)

Animation: Free fall under gravity.

Grade 10 — Electrostatics, Magnetism, Optics, Oscillations

  • Coulomb's Force: \( F = k\dfrac{q_1 q_2}{r^2},\quad k = 9\times10^9 \)
  • Electric field: \( \vec{E} = \dfrac{\vec{F}}{q} \)
  • Potential: \( \varphi = \dfrac{A}{q} \)
  • Capacitance: \( C = \dfrac{q}{U} \)
  • Ampere force: \( F = BIl\sin\alpha \)
  • Lorentz force: \( \vec{F} = q[\vec{v}\times\vec{B}] \)
  • Faraday's law: \( \mathcal{E} = -\dfrac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t},\quad \Phi = BS\cos\alpha \)
  • Pendulum period: \( T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{l}{g}} \)
  • Spring pendulum: \( T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{m}{k}} \)
  • Refraction: \( \dfrac{\sin i}{\sin r} = n \)
  • Thin lens: \( \dfrac{1}{d} + \dfrac{1}{f} = \dfrac{1}{F} \)

Reactive Energy

In AC circuits, reactive energy is related to phase shift between current and voltage: \( Q = UI\sin\phi \). It circulates between source and load without doing useful work.

Elasticity (დრეკადობა)

Elasticity is the property of materials to return to their original shape after deformation. Hooke's law: \( F = -kx \). Elastic potential energy: \( E = \dfrac{1}{2}kx^2 \).

Animation: Elastic spring deformation.

Collisions (დრეკადობა / Столкновения)

When two bodies collide, the law of conservation of momentum always holds: \[ m_1\vec{v}_1 + m_2\vec{v}_2 = m_1\vec{v}_1' + m_2\vec{v}_2' \]

Elastic Collision (Упругий удар)

Both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved. The balls bounce off each other. If equal masses: they exchange velocities completely.

\[ \frac{m_1 v_1^2}{2} + \frac{m_2 v_2^2}{2} = \frac{m_1 v_1'^2}{2} + \frac{m_2 v_2'^2}{2} \]

Perfectly Inelastic Collision (Неупругий удар)

The bodies stick together and move as one. Momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is partially lost (converted to heat/deformation).

\[ m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)\,v' \quad\Rightarrow\quad v' = \frac{m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2}{m_1 + m_2} \]

Elasticity (დრეკადობა)

Elasticity is the property of a body to return to its original shape after deformation is removed. Described by Hooke's law: \( F = -kx \). The elastic potential energy stored: \[ E_{el} = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 \]

Animation: Spring deformation and recovery (დრეკადობა).

Reactive Motion (Реактивное движение)

Reactive motion occurs when a body ejects part of its mass — the reaction force propels the body forward. No external force needed. Examples: rockets, jet engines, squid.

Conservation of Momentum

Initially the system is at rest: \(\vec{p} = 0\). After ejecting gas:

\[ m_{\text{rocket}}\,\vec{v}_{\text{rocket}} + m_{\text{gas}}\,\vec{v}_{\text{gas}} = 0 \]

The rocket and exhaust move in opposite directions — total momentum stays zero.

Examples in Nature & Tech

  • 🚀 Rocket / Space launch — combustion gases ejected downward
  • ✈️ Jet aircraft — hot gases ejected backward
  • 🦑 Squid / Octopus — ejects water to move
  • 🎈 Balloon — air escapes, balloon flies opposite direction
